**Muhyiddin Ibn al-Arabi** (1165–1240 CE) is arguably the most influential and controversial figure in the history of Islamic mysticism (Sufism). Known to his followers as **Al-Shaykh al-Akbar** ("The Greatest Master"), he was a visionary, a poet, and a philosopher who systematized Sufi thought into a complex metaphysical framework.

Here is a comprehensive overview of his life, his major works, and his profound teachings.

---

### 1. Biography: The Life of a Traveler

Ibn al-Arabi’s life was characterized by constant physical travel and ceaseless spiritual exploration.

**Early Life in Al-Andalus**
*   **Birth:** He was born in **Murcia, Al-Andalus (modern-day Spain)** in 1165.
*   **Youth:** His father was a government official, and Ibn al-Arabi received a standard education. However, in his adolescence, he experienced a profound spiritual calling. He retreated into seclusion, experiencing visions that gave him knowledge without formal study.
*   **The Meeting with Averroes (Ibn Rushd):** A famous anecdote describes a teenage Ibn al-Arabi meeting the great Aristotelian philosopher Ibn Rushd. The philosopher asked the boy if mystical illumination revealed the same truths as rational philosophy. Ibn al-Arabi replied, "Yes and No." This signaled his lifelong stance: reason has limits, but mystical intuition (*kashf*) provides direct access to divine truth.

**The Journey East**
*   Around the age of 30, following a vision telling him to go to the East, he left Spain, never to return. He traveled through North Africa (Tunisia, Egypt), meeting various Sufi masters.
*   **Mecca:** He arrived in Mecca in 1202. Here, he began writing his magnum opus, *The Meccan Revelations*. He also met a young Persian woman named Nizam, whose beauty and wisdom inspired his famous collection of love poetry, *The Interpreter of Desires*. He viewed her as a manifestation of Divine Wisdom (Sophia).

**Final Years in Damascus**
*   After traveling through Anatolia (Turkey), Iraq, and Palestine, he settled in **Damascus, Syria**.
*   He spent his final years teaching and writing. He died in 1240 CE. His tomb, located in the Salihiyya district of Damascus, remains a major site of pilgrimage today.

---

### 2. Core Teachings and Philosophy

Ibn al-Arabi’s writing is dense, symbolic, and often paradoxical. He aimed to force the mind to break free of linear logic to grasp the Infinite.

#### A. *Wahdat al-Wujud* (The Unity of Being)
This is the concept most associated with him, though he did not use this exact term (his followers coined it).
*   **The Concept:** There is only one Reality: God. Nothing exists independently of God. The created universe is not God (pantheism), but it is a reflection or a shadow of God.
*   **The Mirror:** Ibn al-Arabi often used the metaphor of a mirror. The universe is a mirror in which God contemplates His own names and attributes. You are not the reflection in the mirror, but without you, the reflection wouldn't exist.

#### B. The Divine Names
Ibn al-Arabi taught that the cosmos is a manifestation of the "99 Names of God" (e.g., The Merciful, The Avenger, The Creator, The Patient).
*   Every phenomenon in the universe is a manifestation of a specific Divine Name.
*   Creation exists because God desired to be known ("I was a hidden treasure, and I loved to be known, so I created the creation").

#### C. *Al-Insan al-Kamil* (The Perfect Human)
If the universe is a large mirror, the "Perfect Human" is the polish on that mirror that allows God to see Himself perfectly.
*   **Microcosm:** The human being contains all the attributes of the universe in miniature.
*   **The Bridge:** The Perfect Human acts as an "isthmus" (*barzakh*) between the Divine and the earthly.
*   While all humans have the *potential* to be perfect, the prophets and great saints have actualized this potential. For Ibn al-Arabi, the Prophet Muhammad is the ultimate archetype of the Perfect Human.

#### D. Imagination (*Khayal*)
Unlike modern definitions of imagination as "fantasy" or "unreal," Ibn al-Arabi viewed the **Imaginal World** (*Alam al-Mithal*) as a real layer of existence located between the spiritual and the physical.
*   This is the realm where dreams, visions, and angels exist.
*   He argued that one must use "creative imagination" to perceive God, as God transcends simple rational categorization.

#### E. The "Religion of Love"
Ibn al-Arabi is famous for his universalist approach to spirituality. He believed that since all things are manifestations of God, all worship is ultimately directed toward God, even if the worshiper doesn't realize it.
*   **Famous Verse:**
    > *"My heart has become capable of every form:*
    > *A pasture for gazelles and a convent for Christian monks,*
    > *And a temple for idols and the pilgrim's Kaa'ba,*
    > *And the tables of the Torah and the book of the Quran.*
    > *I follow the religion of Love: whatever way Love's camels take,*
    > *that is my religion and my faith."*

### 3. Major Works

He wrote hundreds of books, but three stand out as essential:

1.  **Al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya (The Meccan Revelations):**
    *   A massive encyclopedia of Sufi knowledge (thousands of pages). It covers metaphysics, Islamic law, cosmology, autobiography, and dream interpretation. It is his "summa theologica."

2.  **Fusus al-Hikam (The Bezels of Wisdom):**
    *   His most focused and controversial metaphysical work. It consists of 27 chapters, each dedicated to a specific Prophet (Adam, Noah, Abraham, Jesus, Muhammad, etc.).
    *   He explains the specific "wisdom" or aspect of the Divine that each Prophet represents. For example, Jesus represents the wisdom of Prophecy and Breath (life-giving).

3.  **Tarjuman al-Ashwaq (The Interpreter of Desires):**
    *   A collection of love poetry. While on the surface they appear to be romantic poems about a woman (Nizam), Ibn al-Arabi wrote a commentary explaining that every line refers to spiritual truths and the longing of the soul for God.

---

### 4. Controversy and Criticism

Ibn al-Arabi has always been a polarizing figure in Islamic history.

*   **The Critics:** Orthodox theologians (most notably **Ibn Taymiyyah**) accused him of heresy. They argued that his concept of *Wahdat al-Wujud* blurred the distinction between the Creator and the created, potentially leading to the idea that God is the physical world (pantheism), which contradicts the Islamic doctrine of God's transcendence.
*   **The Defenders:** His supporters argue that critics misunderstand his complex terminology. They maintain that he was a devout Muslim who strictly followed Sharia (law) but possessed a higher spiritual insight that cannot be understood through logic alone.

### 5. Legacy

Ibn al-Arabi’s influence is immeasurable.
*   **Sufi Orders:** Almost all later Sufi orders adopted his terminology and metaphysical worldview.
*   **Poetry:** He heavily influenced the great Persian poets like Iraqi and Jami (and indirectly Rumi).
*   **Philosophy:** His integration of philosophy and mysticism laid the groundwork for the "School of Isfahan" and thinkers like Mulla Sadra.
*   **Modern Era:** Today, he is studied intensely in the West by scholars of comparative religion and psychology, who see in his work a sophisticated system of psycho-cosmology and religious tolerance.

In summary, Ibn al-Arabi represents the intellectual peak of Islamic mysticism. He sought to explain how the Many (the universe) relates to the One (God) and how the human being serves as the bridge between the two.
